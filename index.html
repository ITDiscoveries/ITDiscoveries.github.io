About

Hi, My name is Colin Adreani

I am a student at the John Martinson Honors College at Purdue studying Aerospace Engineering, and, this summer, I will be going on a study away trip called "Italian Discoveries." Though my continental Italian heritage is distant (A few generations back) I established a link to it through culinary exploration and actual exploration through the Tuscan countryside.

I see Italy as a very important part of Western history; it is the birthplace of the Roman Empire, the cradle renaissance, and the center of the Catholic world. Its cultural exports are seen around the world in fashion, cuisine, and music. The cities we are visiting, Venice, Milan, and Florence, exemplify this with their historical importance given the city-states that were based around them and their role in the renaissance, especially given that Leonardo Da Vinci visited all three. The art and architecture housed in these cities serve as an important historical record of the time and serve as inspiration all over the world. Italian cuisine is of course one of the main things I think about. Cooking is one of my main hobbies, so the flavors of the country have always had a special place in my heart. Much of Italian dining culture is very different from what we have in the states and is often replicated and imitated elsewhere.

I hope to deepen my knowledge about the cultures that still survive here and the histories and stories that led the world to its current moment. Because of the expansive influence that Italy and the Renaissance had on the course of Western culture, thought, and technology, I want to know the social and philosophical factors that led to that storm of innovation that the Renaissance is famous for. Given that the Renaissance was the "rebirth" of classical thought, I want to know why that period of history interested them so much and how those influences came through in their worldviews.

If you want to contact me, you can reach me at italiandiscoveries2026@gmail.com.

A look into Leonardo's Ornithopter

“Starting with Galileo and the seventeenth century, extension was substituted for localization” (Foucault, 1986). In our postmodern society, nothing is more important than traveling fast, which means to do so without hindrances like the uneven ground given by mountains and hills or waves and torrential winds to knock the boats we sail. We yearn to extend our reach to all reaches of the earth, and to avoid all these distractions from our goal of ultimate expansion, humans turned to the sky. We have been obsessed with flight since the time of the ancients, with Daedalus and Icarus being prime examples of humanity's obsession with reaching for the heavens. In the story, bird feathers are plucked and used to fashion wings from wax, showing how the first ways humans ever conceived of flight was through the imitation of nature.

Leonardo’s development of the ornithopter started likely with an intense study into the flight mechanics of birds. He has a long history of deriving mechanics using human bodies as inspiration (Museo Galileo), and in 1505-1506, he published a full codex all about the flight of birds (Jakab, 2013). While the original drawings for the ornithopter came before, in his Manuscript B from 1487-90 (Museo Galileo), the knowledge he wrote in the Codex on the Flight of Birds was clearly present in this earlier stage in life. Da Vinci spent a lot of time spread out over years conceptualizing the different ways he could make a man fly, with initial designs of the winged flight going back to 1478 (Museo Galileo). These were heavily based on the wings of a bird and serve as some of the first and most famous examples of biomimicry. This likely draws on the Renaissance belief that efficiency and perfection comes from symmetry and mathematics. Leonardo was one of the people to notice nature’s tendency to include these factors, especially in his studies in the human body where he hypothesised “perfect” ratios that form the human body which are reflected across our Sagittal plane. This all culminates in the ornithopter, a flapping flying machine inspired by bird flight which would theoretically allow for humans to take to the sky. In the centuries after Da Vinci’s death, people would try to bring it into reality, and many succeeded in making them fly without humans, instead using springs, rubber bands, and even explosive power (Ornithopter Society). While human powered ornithopter flight remains highly impractical and inefficient, the advances Leonardo Da Vinci made in aerodynamics and flight paved the way for the Wright brothers and eventually the postmodern, hypermobile society we live in today.

Da Vinci’s ornithopter was special in that it was a vehicle. Many earlier designs and mythical stories conceptualized a human’s winged flight as a suit or wings affixed to one's arms, and other winged flying machines lacked humans altogether, abandoning the dream of taking to the sky. To generate lift, Da Vinci’s ornithopter incorporated wings inspired by birds with “an impressive wingspan exceeding 33 feet, constructed with a frame made of pine and covered in raw silk—a combination that aimed for a delicate balance between lightness and sturdiness. The pilot’s position was prone, lying face down on a board at the centre of the invention.” (Civil Aviation Academy, 2024). The human operator simply needed to operate a series of levers and pulleys that would cause the wings to flap in the same manner a bird does. The operator could use their feet to control the angle of the wings, while using their arms to generate a flapping motion. With this, a well trained and incredibly strong man should have been able to fly. Its genius comes in its simplicity. Very few mechanical linkages still give its operator total control over the position and movement of the wings.

The Ornithopter was not alone in Da Vinci’s designs, as much of his body of work was dedicated to methods of movement and war. These other designs for flight included the Ariel Screw, which generated lift like a fan does by pushing air downwards continuously. This type of rotary design was, like many of the things Da Vinci did, centuries ahead of its time, as the same kind of motion is used today for drones and helicopters. This came during his time as a military consultant for Milan (Museo Galileo), and it was likely meant to be paired with various explosives or artillery for aerial bombardment. It is not hard to imagine how Leonardo could have used the Ornithopter for war as well, to deploy troops behind enemy walls was an unheard of and incredibly powerful tactic at that time.

A deep dive into Campo San Zaccaria

This is an aedicule that frames a door with a swan neck pediment atop it.

Around it is this blind arcade lining the outside of a building next to the church. The spaces between the columns are called bays.

A campanile sits next to the church in the more gothic section.

The gothic section of church includes corbels connecting the roof to the walls.

The apse of the Church of San Zaccaria is most clear on this diagram placed at the entrance of the church. It sits at the end of the nave, or the central approach.

dentilled molding is shown here lining the bottom of a cornice.

Along the whole church face, pilasters appear to support the structures above them.

At the front of the church is another aedicule, this time with a segmental pediment.

This is an Ionic order column, distinguished by the volutes on the capital. Very typical of classic or neoclassic architecture.

The interior of the church includes these biforate windows that each include cusps giving them the uniquely gothic curved shape. They also incorporate quatrefoils, another distinct gothic piece.


“It is only apparently cocks that are fighting there. Actually, it is men” (Geertz 60). There is always more under the surface when observing the curious habits of humanity. While on the surface, Campo San Zaccaria seems to be just a normal little courtyard built around a church, when one spends enough time in Venice, they realize that no Campo is the same, and they each hold stories to tell. By diving into how the populace uses the space, going about their day, and, in the case of San Zaccaria, how tourists play into this dynamic, one can learn more about the interpersonal relationships built in the campo and the way it feeds into the culture of Venice as a whole.

Perhaps the most important part of any campo is the church. Most campos in Venice have some sort of church in them as many were built where they are precisely because of the church’s presence. “Contemporary space is perhaps still not entirely desanctified … And perhaps our life is still governed by a certain number of oppositions that remain inviolable” (Foucault 23). Even though Catholicism is still the dominant religion in the country by far, many people do not regularly attend church, and the number of those who identify as Catholic is dropping. Despite this, the churches that center a campo and give it their name still drive much of the community in these areas. In the case of San Zaccaria, it is home to one of the great works of Giovanni Bellini, titled “Madonna Enthroned with Child and Saints,” which draws many art enthusiasts and students alike to marvel at its 3D illusion effect. This has positioned San Zaccaria as one of the campos that is most used as a stop on a larger journey.

If one is to sit and observe for a whole day the people that come through San Zaccaria, I doubt they would have much luck chatting with any of them. There are never any children at play or elderly couples exchanging flowers on a bench, just a calm quiet interrupted by the occasional bark of a corgi wearing a black harness or the horn of a ship calling all cruisers to the shore once more. The brilliant white stones that form the columns of the church’s facade begin to reflect the sun back into the eyes of anyone there in the middle hours of the day. It bathes passersby in its own divine light in sharp contrast to the gothic brick of the old church. The burnt red bell tower looms over you next to the small garden in the corner that brings flashes of green life to a viewer. Opposite the church is a row of shops: a bar, gelato, leather goods, jewelry, and clothing side by side. There is no seating, anywhere; the closest you may get is the closed off well that sits in the middle. The only bit of shade is provided by a large tree hanging out of the garden that reaches out to the people of Venice with kind arms to protect the skin of those caught under its leaves. When passing through the campo, you are never alone. It is an expressway for tourists and me to get from our docking location to the inner workings of the city. The last time I visited, just passing through, my forward progress was halted by a mobbing family of other American tourists slowly strolling along their merry way. As they licked their gelato and discussed their trip, I couldn’t help but notice the other tour group in front of us as well. Every person I saw in that campo that day was a visitor just as I was. Although I was irritated by this deceleration, I recognized that I occupied the same social position they did. I, too, was moving through San Zaccaria as a visitor, just passing through.

The rules are simple and few. To not disturb the natural flow, never stop moving, and live and let live. The only exception would be if you would like to stop in a store, other than that there is no reason, just keep moving. The stream of people is unbothered by your presence, and as such should be treated the same way. Everyone needs to get somewhere, don’t stop them. San Zaccaria is an intermediate for larger things. On one side is the boardwalk and on the other the heart of San Marco. You mustn't linger otherwise you would be subject to foul looks and confusion.

I suspect these rules are due to its location and design. It is located just one turn off of the boardwalk that docks all of the cruise ship visitors and vaporetto riders and is one of the first you may run into when trying to venture your way into the city. As such, many people there are not from there – the only locals to pass through the campo are those taking their dogs for a walk. It being mostly tourists who pass through gives a clue as to how this culture formed. A tourist in a new city, especially one as confusing as Venice, is bound to feel uncomfortable. San Zaccaria stands as one of their first forays into the city and thus puts them on edge. They are drawn to their instincts of keeping to themselves lest they be judged, so they just keep moving. On the other side, the locals passing through have come to resent the tourists for clogging up their streets, so they want nothing to do with them. They just keep moving forward. These opposing and complimentary mindsets of unease and discontent both work to enforce the more modern sensibility that the campo is no destination, just the journey. It is also exacerbated by the lack of seating and shade. Except for the one tree in the shady corner, San Zaccaria is completely barren of places to slow down. The bar just serves coffee on the go, and the gelateria is just a front, no tables or chairs give me and the other tourists a break from our feet. As it stands, despite having some of the most interesting neoclassical architecture and Renaissance art contained within the walls of its church, the campo San Zaccaria stands as a liminal pathway that holds a culture of disinterest. A tourist quickly learns that their instinct was right and they must stay their course, and a local has something better to do. As I stood there watching, no one glanced over at me. It was as if I had become invisible.

Experiencing my first few campos, I thought they would all be similar: hubs of conversation and Venetian life that form the web of navigation through the city. San Zaccaria told me that the way of life propagated by the locals can be almost entirely disrupted by a sudden influx of tourists who cannot read the rules of the space. San Zaccaria does not function like other campos and feels so empty precisely because the tourist mindset has completely replaced the Venetian one. It is an enclosed microcosm of how outside influence can change the way we interact with and live in a space. It motivated me to ensure I take great care to learn the rules by which a city beats so that I can more deeply appreciate all it has to offer.

May 21, 2026 The area around and North of San Marco, Venice

The Scavenger Hunt

Poetry is hard. Layers of double entendres and cultural knowledge are needed to properly understand what it is telling you, and without it one can find themselves absolutely lost and disoriented. This is the situation Dunkin and I found ourselves in today when trying to decipher: “I may be slow, but I climb high / Out of my shell, into the sky / Take a picture with the tower / That symbolized a family’s power.” The exploration of Venice that followed this poetic riddle confronted the comfortability we thought we had with the city and gave me insights into the diverse mindsets of Venetian citizens.

Our analysis started with some guesswork on the meaning behind the puzzle. In our previous ventures into the city, we noticed that many family coat of arms adorned the walls of family homes, so we figured that we had to look for a tower associated with a family whose coat of arms could be figured out by the rest of the clue. The first idea was that a turtle was somehow incorporated into the symbolism, possibly as a show of strength and stability. We thought that only a rich family would be able to afford a tower with their symbol on it, so we focused our efforts on the area between San Marco’s Plaza and the Rialto, as this is where many aristocratic families would have lived. We traveled through various Campos looking to get a general idea of the area and to find people we had met before to ask them. At the restaurant Osteria Alla Staffa, our waiter affirmed our suspicion that the coat of arms were important and confirmed that the Rialto area had a larger concentration of them. We then wandered to a guitar shop Dunkin had visited and encountered what would become an enduring trouble in our search. The man, David, that Dunkin had conversed with the other day, while still friendly, was not willing to help us as he was very busy with rearranging the shop. Many locals would, like David, not be very helpful because they were either in a hurry or just wanted to enjoy their time in peace and quiet– for example one man told me, “Can’t you see I’m eating here!”. Perhaps I should not be so surprised that the Venetian populace has adapted some of these more modern ideas of speed. As Vrillio writes, “The fleet in being creates a new dromocratic idea: the notion of displacement without destination in space and time” (64) and, “Speed as a pure idea without content comes from the sea” (68). It makes sense for a naval empire to have the seeds for a speedy and rapid way of life, wanting to stay unbothered on their quests to move from place to place. While wanting to be unbothered in their movement is one aspect of the Venetian approach to stranger interaction, another important aspect is how they appreciate the smaller points in life. They are more inclined to cherish their little lunch breaks and the peace and quiet it grants them, so interruption would be akin to waking a meditating monk. Though opposite, these two mindsets both hindered our search for the tower, so we decided to shift our focus away from people in shops we had been in.

Instead of being a bother, we wanted to approach people who seemed to have more time on their hands. We started in the area in front of the Church of St Mary of Miracles, where we found someone who looked to be a student sitting around just admiring the neoclassical work. She turned out to have only been in Venice for a few days, but her and her dad said the wording about the shell reminded them of the Birth of Venus painting. This mirrored other interpretations of that part, where other people had mentioned Venus, but they pointed out that the clocktower in San Marco’s Plaza used to have the planet Venus on it. While we remained unconvinced that the clocktower was correct, it showed us that we had gotten too stuck with the idea of a turtle, so, after some confirmation that it was not the clocktower, we continued our search a little further from the touristy areas, and closer to where students lived as many of them might just be hanging out. When asking people who spoke Italian, they were absolutely bewildered at what it could mean, even when reading it in both Italian and English. We realized at this point that while looking out for a coat of arms might still be one avenue to the answer, we were more lost than we realized. At this point we had been turning around and around so often, we lost all sense of direction and place. We sat in a random Campo next to a seagull and gathered our thoughts.

Our Professore granted us a gift: an alternate Italian translation of the hint. It read, “Forse sono lento, ma salgo in alto / Fuori dal mio bovolo, verso il cielo spicco il salto. / Scatta una foto con la torre spirale / Che simboleggia il potere di un’antica casata ducale.” This gave us a few hints, even by just comparing to the previous language. The first part was the same, and translatable with my passing knowledge of Spanish, but the rest seemed important. The two words that stuck out in comparison were bovolo, spirale, and ducale. Spirale was relatively easy, as it is a very close cognate to “Spiral,” and ducale was one we had seen before in relation to the Doge of Venice, so that confirmed our suspicion of an aristocratic family. All this helped, but bovolo still confused us. After this hint and a little exploration, we came across a woman who was willing to help and seemed to live in Venice and speak very good English. She told us that a bovolo is a type of Venetian snail that lends its name to a spiral staircase. She was helpful enough to lead us to it as she was heading home which was very close to it, and we turned into a part of Venice that Dunkin and I had walked through just a couple hours earlier. We had turned off the main antpath of people that was filled with 6-7’ing elementary schoolers to get away from the noise, and ended up at this art exhibit inside of an old Venetian Gothic building of red brick and stone detailing with a small garden and well in front. Lo and behold, this was the exact building we were looking for, as its main prominent feature was the elegant spiral staircase on the outside that connected its three floors. The woman who guided us also happened to be going to the exhibit, but it was not free to enter so we simply headed back to San Servolo after a five hour epic to locate this staircase and each promptly took a nap.

The troubles we had seemed as if they were cultivated by the environment we were exploring. De Certeau writes about the modern city in The Practice of Everyday Life: “The production of its own space: rational organization must thus repress all the physical, mental and political pollutions that would compromise it” (94). It follows that the Medieval city would have departed from this ideal in nearly every way. A Medieval city is not organized or rational, it sprawls based where people thought it would be best to plop down their house. Unlike the modern city, based on an impartial grid that stops at nothing, Venice looks from above as a patchwork thing that is sure to intimidate others. Only those who have grown accustomed to it or grew up in it can appreciate the physical and mental benefits that the organization – or lack thereof – brings. Achieving the goal of the city requires an intimate connection with it, something we do not see in the more postmodern cities which lend themselves to impartial and direct navigation. Only by plunging yourself into this beautiful mess of short narrow pathways and campos peppered ever few compounds can one appreciate the beauty of Venice and stop feeling so much like a foreign tourist.

May 23, 2026 Giardini della Biennale, Venice

Postmodern Art

Visually striking and taking up a whole wall in one of the most prestigious art exhibits in the world, these works from The Affirmative Happiness of a Green Dot, by Hala Schoukair, caught my eye almost instantly. The eerily biological forms spread across the canvases can invoke feelings of disgust, intrigue, and wonder. There is no doubt that these works are indicative of many of the hallmarks of postmodern thought and experience.

The first thing many might notice is the color. The yellowish and greenish hues are precisely the range of colors our eyes are best evolved to see at. This color choice calls the audience to it, especially against its more plain and complimentary colored back wall. The color also plays into its biological imagery, as many speculate the reason we evolved such a sensitivity in this color range is that it allowed us to distinguish objects and animals in nature more effectively, a very biological and evolutionary reason for this phenomenon. Renaissance artists were no stranger to extravagant colors, one need only look at Paolo Veronese’s wonderful blues and reds that pop right off of a page to know this, but they very rarely dabbled in monochromatic compositions while painting – they always depicted whole scenes full of people or vegetation. This, on the other hand, has just one color theme for each painting, and as a whole they are all very analogous.

This is a result of the choice of subject. While at first appearing to be abstract shapes, just as when “Looking at a bubble-chamber photograph, the student sees confused and broken lines, the physicist a record of familiar subnuclear events” (Kuhn 111), anyone who has placed a leaf under a microscope would recognize the distinct plant cell shape exhibited by the artworks on the right. The two on the left, similarly, appear to look like a picture of pollen or eggs on a leaf and other micro and just above life. Reading the statement sat next to it, these suspicions are confirmed. The art stems from the walks the artist would take with their mother and her teachings of examining and appreciating the little things, the movement and art in nature. This focus on nature and especially the tiny is a natural extension of Renaissance practices. Painters like Leonardo Da Vinci pioneered the idea of desanctifying art in favor of a more humanistic approach. They believed in the beauty of the human form, and what Schoukair is portraying here takes that philosophy and applies it to everything. All life on this planet is beautiful and deserves celebration, even if you can’t see it with just your eyes. As a departure from Renaissance ideals, it completely abandons the idea of the human form, but also echoes the Renaissance’s obsession with scientific discovery. Glass, the invention that let us see into this microscopic world, was perfected right there in Venice, making it only fitting for this to be displayed here.

Another composition-based effect of the subject matter is the near complete lack of depth. Each cell and particle exists atop an unfocused background of biology. It recalls the old ideas of anagogic space, where divine figures were placed on a flat golden background to show their separation from mortal existence. Here, the creatures of earth are seemingly placed in this space, elevating their experiences to that of the divine. This also exists as a departure from the Renaissance realism push, “an approximation at least to that photographic accuracy against which modern artists have rebelled” (Gombrich 34). While typical of many postmodern art pieces, this effective disregard for 3D space is not necessarily seen here. In this fantastical world of cells and microbes, the world is seen through the eyes of science. In the same way that Renaissance masters embraced the camera obscura as their method of displaying the world, Schoukair has chosen the microscope. Again, we see an extension of Renaissance techniques adapted for a postmodern sensibility.

One of the most interesting aspects is the border and positioning. There is no ornate frame or special attention given to this artwork in particular. As opposed to the cartoon of Raphael’s School of Athens, which is hidden behind multiple doorways and hallways in a dramatically lit room, Schoukair’s work appears almost casual. In the Renaissance, “the painter, unlike ordinary mortals, is a person endowed with the divine gift of perceiving, not the imperfect and shifting world of individuals, but the eternal patterns themselves” (Gombrich 155), but here we do not see that. The art is meant to be read simply and the viewer is supposed to create a personal connection with it rather than marveling at its complexities. No ornate frame tells us this is more important than anything else, it is just there and it is beautiful.

All of these subtle rejections and extensions of Renaissance art indicate an early placement in the transition towards a postmodern paradigm. They indicate a new mindset that values personal experience rather than objective truth, as the paintings invite a personal connection both from the artist and viewer; art is becoming more egalitarian rather than closed off to be enjoyed just by the rich and powerful. Human senses are no longer infallible, as they can be surpassed by technology and deceived by it, and at the same time human experiences have never been so important. The little things we experience as people are what make our life great, no divine order can change that for us. Art and knowledge are for everyone and anyone is capable of anything.

May 24, 2026 Milan, Italy

The difference in space and pace between Medieval and Postmodern cities

The two ambulances that drove past our hotel in Milan were a comforting and jarring departure from the serene calm of San Servolo and the waterways of Venice. Within Italy, you would be hard pressed to create as a dramatic juxtaposition between the medieval and postmodern worlds as the one we had, going from Venice which has no cars or really motors inside the city, to the concrete jungles that surround Milan and the smells of gasoline and burning rubber that fill the air around Sforza’s castle and the Duomo. From Venice’s slow waves crashing against our Vapporetto and calm breezes to the honking horns and aforementioned sirens. As it may be clear, I think the best way to understand the differences between these two paradigms is through the comparison of how people move through the space and their ways of transport, especially since the road/pathway layout of both cities is still decidedly medieval.

Pace in Venice seemed sort of slow and methodical because of its reliance on human-powered transit within the city. While getting from one side of the island to another can be done with Vaporetti, it is often more direct and more scenic to take the footpaths through the city instead. In these, congested traffic blocks like we see on the freeways in the USA are replaced by long sprawling lines of tourists that our professore calls the “ant paths.” A fun phenomenon created by the outsiders sticking almost exclusively to these turbulent streams of bodies is that a single turn away can bring you to a quaint neighborhood with just the occasional local going about your day which brings with it a sense of calm and quiet as you become isolated from the masses. It is here that you will likely find many of the best places in Venice. It is home to many calmer campos with ample seating and the sounds of youth playing and dogs barking at seagulls for their pizza. These places are only accessible by foot and serve to slow you down and orient you in space. While many visitors see just the antpaths and associate Venice with that hustle and bustle with people just crawling along while trying to move as fast as possible, a couple steps away is a slice of life unaffected by the modern attitudes about speed and efficiency. Restaurants here only serve their local area, as everyone just walks, so they are unconcerned with turning tables as quick as possible or generating the most profit, they instead seat just 10 tables with some of the best homestyle food you could ever ask for, The bars are not places to get drunk and leave, instead they serve you sitting down so you can talk with your friends just a 5 minute walk away from your bed. Part of this more confined lifestyle, in stark contrast to our more boisterous typical American lifestyle, is likely a result of what Foucault calls “the space of emplacement” (Foucault 22). Medieval cities are walled off and enclosed, emplaced in this area unable to expand. They are sustained by the slower foot traffic that is essentially forced upon you in Venice and other medieval style cities, creating a slower and more relaxed pace of life, separate from the post-Renaissance mandate for expansion.

The transportation to and through Milan indicates the shift in perspective the world has seen since the industrial revolution. The train we took made rivers and mountains move past our windows in seconds and minutes. Getting from the train station to our hotel involved not a leisurely walk across the cobbles of Milan, but a descent into the tunnels of the Metro. In newer parts of Milan such as Porta Nuovo have highrise glass skyscrapers looming over the terracotta roofs of the residences next to them. As opposed to the foot traffic of Venice, Milan relies heavily on motor vehicles and mass transit systems. In "Of Other Spaces", Foucault also writes, “We are in the epoch of juxtaposition, the epoch of the near and far, of the side-by-side, of the dispersed” (Foucault 22). This more modern and postmodern mindset that Foucault describes encapsulates Milan perfectly. As a whole, Italy has held on to much of its Renaissance roots, but Milan has become the biggest breakaway. It juxtaposes the new and old in a single picture frame, and by ways of its metro, disperses its people as efficiently as possible. Speed has become the hallmark of a city rather than emplacement; hustle and bustle is the default rather than the exception. We see it in how military tactics have evolved to move soldiers faster and faster on land, sea, and air, and we see it in how new developments separate pedestrians and cars, not only for safety of both parties, but so both can move as fast as possible. This is the same reason why faster transit systems have been developing ever since we entered a more modern era, whether that be better roads, bridges, then eventually boats and planes. “The sea is open, the joining of the demos and the element of freedom” (Virilio 61). Possibly best emphasized in the enlightenment teaching of self-determination, this freedom, especially of movement, that we experience on the sea and in the air is how we try to approach all aspects of our life. When routing a path through a city, most do not consider the most scenic route, the one with the best shops, or anything else other than: “What gets us there the fastest?” This is why the Metro in Milan gets so crowded when a couple stops could easily be a 15 minute walk; speed is simply everything.

May 29, 2026 A train from Florence to Venice, Italy

Something Beautiful

The games we play together are gateways to the most beautiful interactions. As a group of people all work together towards a common goal, their hearts meld together and they can begin to understand the inner workings of each other’s minds a little bit more. This was my thought one day in Campo Santa Margherita sitting eating pizza purchased from a local shop. While the food itself was delicious, perfectly crispy bottom, slightly sweet sauce with a salty cheese melted over it. The acidity of the tomatoes cutting through the fat and melding together to create a beautiful dance of flavor as we watched life go by. Dunkin and I had just recently finished playing a game of hacky sack, although unlike other times we were not alone.

Under a tree and next to a flagpole where our friends sat and observed, Dunkin and I started kicking up a little yarn ball. We danced over the cobbled ground, careful not to fall. Whack, Whack, Whack, Groaaan. It was pretty difficult to keep the sack up for any extended period of time, it kept flying off between us or over our heads. It was one of these occasions where the sack happened to roll over next to a little boy in a red shirt. He picked it up and brought it over to us then asked something in Italian. Of course Dunkin and I had no idea the words he was saying, but his intent was clear. The game had transcended language and culture. We tossed it up to him and… splat. It hit the floor as his kick soared away from it. After some time, I asked if he spoke Spanish, to which he said just a little. SUCCESS! We now had a way to teach him how to play. I explained some of the strategy and rules such as keeping your feet low, not using your hands, and, as he soon came to love, headbutting the thing was perfectly legal. It was around this time that his friend came over. He wanted to play as well, so his friend explained to him the rules. After a couple minutes playing, I noticed that he wore a blue Inter Milan jersey, to which I joked, “Forza Juve!” He laughed and tried to say something to me, but I did not understand. All I knew is that it was likely some kind of insult. Once again, love for a game has transcended our language and culture barrier. After Dunkin’s attempts to speak Italian and being met with very confused faces contorted upside down and inside out from the ridiculousness of his remarks, the sun had started to get closer and closer to the buildings that would eventually obscure it completely. The tree that once provided us shade now extended its reach of darkness about 30° away from us, and the mother, who sat under it, told them it was time to go. After their pleas for “Chinque Minuti, Chinque Minuti,” they were allowed to keep playing for just 5 more minutes. Once they had packed up their backpacks with their school work and left to go home, Dunkin, Amanda and Madeline who were there watching, and I went and all got pizza.

The time we spent playing this game with two random people gave us an appreciation for daily life that no other activity could ever come close to. Through the whole process, no videos or pictures were taken, our entire focus and being was placed in this high stakes game of hacky sack. It was beautiful to be able to fully engross myself in the moment and just live normally for a few minutes. So often we are unable to appreciate the fun of games, instead getting too competitive and prideful about our performance, and I am no exception. But here, I rediscovered a passion for the silly hijinks that occur in a street game and the banter that is had when discussing the individual performances. That moment was special. Though the paths of a couple of Purdue engineers and some random kids in Venice would likely never cross again, the one time they did should not and would not be forgotten for, “beauty is fugitive, being frequently found in place to which we may never return or else resulting from rare conjunction of season, light and weather” (Botton 214). We must seek out moments of beauty like this one and fully appreciate them. Fortunately, about a week after that day, I was able to relive a similar experience.

In the mountains surrounding Monterosso Al Mare, there was a little restaurant that nonetheless managed a massive plot of orchards and gardens. The rolling green hills were interrupted by rows of grape vines, lemon trees, and bushes of magic. Their vibrancy was enhanced by the afternoon sun beating down on them, highlighting the stray leaves that poked out just a little bit more. The bright yellow lemons peak through and catch the rays to bounce the sour melody into our eyes. After the constant uphill walk that it took to get up to the restaurant, you might think we all wanted a rest, but no. Next to the lemon orchard stood a large rectangular patch of grass. It was slightly sloped away from the trees with patches of volunteer weeds sprouting up here and there, peppered in splotches of white and yellow. On either end was a large retaining wall that held up the restaurant and its gardens; it was made of grey and brown brick clearly weathered over decades of emplacement. Opposite the grove was a lower wall separating the field from a more forested area, and sitting under a tree, almost inviting us, calling to us, was a volleyball. Before we got to that, however, Professore, Dunkin, and I had more important matters to attend to. We gathered in an equilateral triangle in the direct center of the field, and Dunkin threw the sack into the air. As our mistakes added up and we migrated slowly into the shade of the lemon trees, our coordination improved. No longer were we bumping feet while trying to kick or no one moved as it fell to the floor, we had reached the flow state. The game had taken over and our minds had been linked together. I had never been so engrossed in my surroundings, the weather was calm and warm as the sun gently kissed my skin, and the soft grass made every footstep feel comforting and grounded. Sure we had observers, our classmates who were getting recordings of every mistake, but they did not matter. I was in the moment, enjoying it.

Once we were done with the sack, it was time for the big one. The overinflated and very thin volleyball, clearly meant as a toy on the beach, would become the weapon of choice in this fierce battle about to unfold at the fields of Buranco. This was Italy, it would be blasphemous to play anything other than soccer. It was Dunkin and I vs Professore, the students against the master. As is typical of random pickup games, since the teams were unmatched, Professore was given the advantage of a larger goal to shoot on, but still, the challenge of a lopsided match was difficult to overcome. The game was off, Professore’s ball. He slowly encroached on our half as I backed up, trying to lead him to where I knew Dunkin sat, guarding the goal. Every step, clearly a probe trying to find a weakness to exploit, but I was sure there was no such thing. A sudden kick outwards by Professore sent the ball into the side wall, what I thought was a mistake of control, the ball was extra bouncy afterall. But instead of losing track if it, Professore immediately runs past me, anticipating the bounce. I had been had, he had me right where he needed me, against the wall with my back turned to his eventual path of travel. Despite his move, he was still up against another defender, and Dunkin was able to tackle him and pass to me. We continued like this, back and forth, each trying different tactics to score, but the manpower advantage was too much. Dunkin and I had scored our second goal with no response just before we had to go up for dinner.

The connections made through play and gaming are not replicable anywhere else. Conversation may teach you about the inner workings of someone’s mind, but sport tells you how they are under pressure and in unexpected situations. It is a look into someone's subconscious as their body acts on impulse for the sole purpose of winning. “The connoisseur, therefore, is no longer advised simply to stand back. He should look at the painter's handiwork closely, admire his touch and the magic of his brush which thus conjure up an image” (Gombrich 199), and in this case we were all acting as connoisseurs of each other. Forming better understandings and connections between ourselves, and there is always a beauty in human connection and being able to understand others better. In some cases there is no better way than to pick up a ball and play a game.

June 3, 2026 Physically in Los Angeles, Spiritually in Florence

Food culture in a more pre-renaissance culture

Italy’s cuisine is world renowned, often ranked among the top 3 of peoples favorites and critically acclaimed with many Michelin starred restaurants, and is one of the most widespread, especially in the United States. Like many European countries, much of Italy prioritizes simplicity while using high quality ingredients that are unadulterated to let the natural and fresh flavors shine — of course this is not the case everywhere. This is why it is so often important to eat primarily local ingredients, especially those that are in season. In my case, this means eating as much seafood in Venice as possible. In my first week, I tried my best to never eat at the same place twice so I could explore the depth and variations in Italian dining culture, and while eating, I took note of the pace, ambience, and overall experience that comes along with a typical Venetian dinner. It is important to note that many of the places I visited, while they had many Italians dining alongside us, had many other tourists as well.

The pace of an Italian dinner is very different. The most striking difference that I believe an American would be shocked about is the lack of active waiting by the staff. Other than bringing you a menu and taking your order, the waiter is unlikely to interact with you unless you directly call them over. This naturally slows down the meal, as while engrossed in a conversation, a diner does not always think to call for the check or for more drinks, instead letting the discussion flow. This points to another interesting fact about Italian dining, which is that you are expected to sit for multiple hours, say to stay from 7:00 to 10:00. When exploring the city at this time, you will find many small groups talking with wine and liquor when many Americans would be back in bed and asleep. Virilio writes in Speed and Politics, “In fact, the strategic value of the non-place of speed has definitively supplanted that of place” (Vrilio 149). This is how Americans typically operate. Instead of valuing the space they inhabit and the time they spend there, the focus is instead put on the efficiency by which you may be transported between them. Tables must be flipped at restaurants to generate maximum profit, and the diners must be seated immediately, and they pay then leave at their earliest convenience. There are no lingering thoughts or calm moments, just rapid consumption and movement. The kitchen itself works much the same: hectic and loud, it could be seen as a productive riot. Comparing this to the Italian chefs we met and the dining experiences we had, you were expected to be calm and relaxed. At times when our group got too excited and loud, we were met with stares stabbing through our backs. This was a constant reminder of how we were supposed to enjoy and prolong the meal in quiet conversation rather than leave and loiter on the streets. When we had the pleasure of viewing the kitchens while seated, they appeared calm and relaxed, as they were never swamped with orders coming in from 20 tables at once. Each dish and moment could be mulled over as long as was necessary.

The process of and philosophy about preparing food seems to be much more consistently personal here; it is relatively rare to find any chain restaurant in Venice. Rather than just turning a profit, the people who decide to go into the food industry here seem to be much more concerned with perfecting their specific craft and injecting their passion for the traditions and carrying that on through the course of their life instead of transitioning to a more profit-driven model. A fun anecdote relating to this happened when four of us tried to go to this restaurant three minutes after it opened and were turned away because they did not have enough space. We, and another group that came before us, were told to come back another day. Rather than giving us a wait time and buzzer to tell us when a table is ready, like is common in the US, we simply had to eat elsewhere. As a whole they turned away around 250 euros in those three minutes alone. This harkens back to the tendencies of the Medieval era where, “there was a hierarchic ensemble of places … It was this complete hierarchy, this opposition, this intersection of places that constituted what could very roughly be called medieval space: the space of emplacement” (Foucault 22). The spaces themselves are as sacred as the culinary practices they use. To dilute its and the food's importance would be akin to diluting the soul of oneself. The care is not placed in the generation of infinite profit, the restaurant makes enough to sustain itself and thats enough. Instead, the epitome of dining is in applying the greatest and most minute care into the taste and experience of the food. So what if it takes hours to make sheets and sheets of lasagna and cups of tiramisu, it's all worth it for the personal pleasure and joy the chef and owner gets from this. I noticed this in essentially every delicious restaurant we went to. The more it appeared that we truly understood and enjoyed the food, the better they treated us, even giving us free stuff after our meal as a show of gratitude. One of my favorite days so far involved a trip to the foothills of the Italian Alps where we learned from two master cooks, one who made pasta and one who made pizza. Both of these men embody this perfectly, as they both spoke with such passion and excitement when explaining the sciences behind their craft. Asking thoughtful questions made them light up, getting their questions right was even better. They had simply poured all of their passion into this discipline, and it showed.

Of course, it is important to say that everything that makes an Italian restaurant “Italian” can be found in America. As we become more postmodern, not only do the spaces we inhabit become less centralized – moving away from the city centers of New York and Chicago and towards the interconnected map of nodes that makes up Los Angeles – but our culture does as well. America could be said to have never had a monoculture, but where we are now, we will never have one. Disparate communities in postmodern spaces embrace different philosophies, and each section grows in accordance to this. What makes Italy different is that everywhere has this more pre-Renaissance mindset to some extent, rather than the pockets of pre-Renaissance, modernism, and postmodern ideals that America exhibits. The heightened uniformity of Italy makes the difference. For those who yearn for this slower paced lifestyle and more intentional and personal philosophies, I encourage them to look. It is there.

June 4, 2026 My House, Los Angeles

What just happened?

Italy was so much more than I ever could have hoped for. It wasn’t necessarily the class material, or the art, or the people that made it special, but the combination of all of these factors. Just as the Renaissance would never have happened if the perfect storm of philosophy, art, and science was not brewing, this trip could have gone wrong in so many ways. On a surface level, everything went great, no one was pick pocketed and lost their passport, we didn’t leave anyone in the wrong city, and no one got scammed out of $200. Just below that, however, we can start to see why it worked so well.

Part of what made it exceed my expectations by so much was the more granular knowledge that our Professore taught us. Much of his knowledge was highly localized, but that made it highly specific. Before this, I knew a good bit about Italian culture, but it was mostly national trends and regional differences at the most granular. Being able to get to know Venice specifically so well taught me a lot about how I should go about approaching my own city living. Venice and LA are so incredibly different, and yet I find myself taking lessons from one and applying them to another. In LA, my navigation is based on central areas that I know where they are in relation to each other, such as century city, the marina, the beach, stuff like that. Landmarks. In Venice, I applied this same concept using the campos. As long as I knew which campo I was in and where the next one was, I could get around pretty well. Inversely, during my bus rides to different coffee shops I’ve been doing since I have been back, I have taken extra care to observe the life moving past me. After meeting so many people I am so interested to hear everyone around me’s stories. These moments of travel that I took so much for granted as just the way to the destination have gained a new light and life for me. As I walked in Venice and took in the architecture and specialty of every road, I now take conscious note of the stylistic choices present in every building around me, thinking about how we use our space and the construction of our cities as a whole.

Much of my daily rituals have been reframed in context of the trip. Just the other day, I cooked dinner for my family, which is something I usually do not do. For a while I simply cooked for myself when I had the time, but now I relish in the feeling of pride I get when those I care for enjoy it with me. I also have a renewed sense for exploration. Despite how Venice “constituted what could very roughly be called medieval space: the space of emplacement” (Foucault 22), it still invigorates the curiosity of an outsider. This is an energy that I have brought back with me, wanting to learn the intricacies of all the disparate communities of my home city, so I’ve been going on what is essentially a coffeehouse tour of all the places I can reach via bus travel.

Most of all, I have come to appreciate the postmodern world and how it can be used to connect on a large scale with so many people. While Los Angeles does not meet all the criteria for being a heterotopia, principally that LA does not “presuppose a system of opening and closing that both isolates them and makes them penetrable” (Foucault 26), it still holds true what I think of as the most engaging part of a heterotopia. Los Angeles, being one of the most diverse metro areas on the planet, is “capable of juxtaposing in a single real place several spaces, several sites that are in themselves incompatible” (Foucault 25). Living here has blessed me with the opportunities to meet people from every part of every continent and connect with them about not just our shared experiences in the city, but reach beyond that to a more personal connection. While it can at many times lead to a diffusion of cultures, the mixing and interaction of the global community in one place gives me so many opportunities that many others lack. This realization has made me infinitely grateful for my place in the world and how postmodern ideology has influenced its development and continuation. Los Angeles would not exist as it is without postmodernism, and I could not exist in my current form without Los Angeles.
